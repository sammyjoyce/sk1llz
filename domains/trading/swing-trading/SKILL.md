This skill is for `Stage 2` momentum continuation, not bottom-fishing. Use it to judge whether a chart is early, properly compressed, and still under accumulation, or whether it only looks clean because you are late.

## Read Order

- Before classifying any setup, read the `weekly` chart first and count bases. A pretty daily chart that is really base 4 or 5 on the weekly is often a distribution gift, not a fresh opportunity.
- Before calling a setup `VCP`, read `Setup Taxonomy` and `Breakout Damage` below. `VCP` is a characteristic bundle, not a geometry template.
- Before building a screener or backtest, read `Freedom Calibration`. If you compress this style into two or three naive rules, you will keep the label and lose the edge.
- Do not import mean-reversion, deep-value, or bottom-calling logic into a Minervini task. This style assumes strength should prove itself first.

## Before You Decide, Ask Yourself

- Is this truly `Stage 2`, or am I rationalizing a base that sits under a weak or flattening long-term trend? Minervini's own studies put the overwhelming majority of superperformance moves in `Stage 2`; outside it, the burden of proof is against the trade.
- Is this `early-stage accumulation` or `late-stage exhaustion`? The same vertical action that is bullish after the first leg out of a correction can be distributive on base 4 or 5.
- Where is the real `line of least resistance`? Tight closes, drying volume, and fewer shares offered matter more than whether the drawing "looks like" a VCP.
- What should happen in the next `1-10 sessions` if I am right? Minervini trades are time-sensitive. If the stock cannot act right quickly, the market is already telling you something.
- Where is the `exact invalidation`, not the emotional invalidation? Define the price or violation cluster that proves you wrong before you discuss upside.

## Setup Taxonomy

- `Trend template`: Treat the classic 50/150/200-day template as the price of admission for standard continuation setups, not a guarantee of quality. A stock can pass the template and still be too late-stage, too wide-and-loose, or too crowded.
- `RS behavior`: Prefer a relative-strength line already rising for at least `6 weeks`, ideally `13+`. A good-looking base with a dead RS line is usually a laggard hiding in a strong tape.
- `VCP`: The key feature is `shrinking supply`: shallower pullbacks, tighter weekly closes, and visibly lighter volume as the base matures. If the swings remain wide or volume stays heavy on the way down, the label `VCP` is cosmetic.
- `Cheat entry`: The highest-quality cheats usually form in the `middle third` of a cup-completion area after the stock has stalled for `5-10%` from high to low and then tightened. The best ones often shake slightly below the obvious prior low first to clear weak hands.
- `Low cheat`: This is an aggressive entry in the `lower third` of the pattern. It carries more risk and only makes sense when the stock is proving powerful sponsorship, often in `big-cap IPOs` after a controlled `15-20%` correction. If the IPO has already sunk `50-60%`, the "bargain" is usually damage, not opportunity.
- `Straight-up-from-the-bottom`: A stock that comes directly from the lows into a breakout without a cheat, handle, or tight pause is far more failure-prone because supply was never tested.
- `Power play`: This is not a normal breakout. Think `100%+ advance in <=8 weeks` on forceful volume, followed by `3-6 weeks` of unusually tight digestion with a correction typically no worse than about `20%` to `25%`. In this pattern, explosive price/volume can matter more than near-term fundamentals, but only if the digestion stays tight.
- `Distance from lows`: Do not reject a leader for already being `100%-300%` above its 52-week low. Minervini-style entries are usually not cheap; they are proven.
- `Base count`: The first and second proper bases are where the asymmetry usually lives. By base `4` or `5`, especially if valuation has already expanded dramatically versus the first base, you should think `distribution risk` first and fresh swing `second`.

## Entry Procedure

1. Start on the `weekly` chart: confirm `Stage 2`, count bases, and reject anything obviously `wide-and-loose`.
2. Move to the `daily` chart: look for `tight closes`, `inside days`, and a clear `volume dry-up` into the pivot area.
3. Classify the entry path as `handle`, `cheat`, `low cheat`, or `power play`. If you cannot say which one it is, you do not understand the setup yet.
4. Mark the exact price where the setup is wrong. For quality Minervini entries, actual losses often land closer to `4-5%`; needing the full `7-8%` allowance repeatedly usually means the entries are too loose.
5. Decide what "on schedule" means. A good breakout should show character quickly; a trade that drifts or reverses immediately needs far less patience than investors want to give it.

## Decision Tree

- If the stock is not clearly `Stage 2`, skip it. Minervini does not need you to be clever below the long-term trend.
- If the weekly chart says base `1-2` and the daily shows real `dry-up`, classify the exact entry type and work from there.
- If the pattern is `base 4-5`, still tight, and extended after a long run, assume `sell-into-strength candidate` before you assume `fresh swing`.
- If the chart is powerful but has no pause, do not chase; wait for a `cheat`, `handle`, or tight `3-6 week` digestion.
- If it might be a `power play`, require the full context: `100%+` thrust, short digestion, and shallow correction. Without that full stack, it is just an extended stock wearing an exciting label.
- If a breakout is only `okay`, not `right`, shrink or exit. The fallback in this style is almost never "give it more room"; it is "be less involved until the stock proves itself."

## Breakout Damage

- `Squat after breakout`: In a healthy bull tape, a breakout that stalls or even turns slightly red on day one is not automatically a failure. Minervini allows `1-10 days` for a `reversal recovery` if the stock does not violate key support or stack fresh warnings.
- `Violation cluster`: Start de-risking when you see multiple problems together: low-volume move out of the base followed by heavy-volume reversal back in, several `lower lows` in a row, more bad closes than good ones, or a decisive close below the `20-day`.
- `50-day logic`: Once the `50-day` has risen to your entry or better, you can use it as a structural sell line instead of an arbitrary trailing percent. In stronger trends Minervini may even wait for the `weekly close`, not the first intraday poke.
- `Climax logic`: A vertical run with `70%+ up days` over `7-15 sessions` or a violent gap can be bullish if it is the `first-stage run` out of a major correction. The same action later in the cycle is often exhaustion. Context outranks the candle.
- `Free roll / back stop`: At roughly `2R-3R`, Minervini will often sell part, move the rest to breakeven or a protected-profit back stop, and force the remainder to earn the right to stay. The stop is chosen by the amount of profit worth protecting, not by mindless tick-for-tick trailing.

## Market-Regime Adjustments

- In weak or sloppy markets, do not respond by giving trades more room. Minervini tightens risk, often toward `5-6%`, takes quicker `10-12%` profits, reduces exposure, and gets off margin.
- In supportive tapes, you can be more willing to sit through a brief `squat` or hold a leader to the `50-day` instead of micromanaging every red day.
- If earnings, news, or serial gap behavior make the chart prone to `overnight air pockets`, treat that as structural risk, not noise. Size down or skip it.

## NEVER

- NEVER buy `straight up from the bottom` because the move feels powerful and missing it feels worse than paying up. Without a cheat, handle, or tight pause, supply was never tested, so the first real selling wave often tags you immediately. Instead wait for a controlled pause that proves shares are no longer being dumped.
- NEVER call something `VCP` because the swings visually narrow. That is seductive because pattern-matching is easier than reading supply. The consequence is buying `wide-and-loose` junk with no real dry-up. Instead demand lighter volume, tighter closes, and visibly shallower contractions.
- NEVER buy base `4` or `5` the same way you buy base `1` or `2` because the chart still looks orderly. Late-stage bases lure you in with prior winners' reputation, then trap you in distribution. Instead downgrade conviction as base count and prior valuation expansion climb.
- NEVER widen stops in a bad tape because volatility makes every chart look unfair. That feels sophisticated, but it usually converts a low-expectancy environment into larger losses. Instead tighten risk, shrink size, and lower profit expectations until the tape improves.
- NEVER auto-sell the first `squat` after breakout because seeing red near the pivot is emotionally loud. That mistake ejects you just before the `reversal recovery` that strong leaders often print in good markets. Instead judge the next `1-10 days` and only act fast if support breaks or violations stack.
- NEVER treat `climactic action` as one rule because big up days look universally dangerous. The consequence is selling early-stage leaders that are actually under heavy accumulation. Instead ask whether the stock is in a first-stage run from a fresh turn or a tired late-stage advance.
- NEVER fall in love with `cheapness` below the `200-day` because it looks like superior risk/reward. In this style, that is usually where serial gappers and trapped supply live. Instead wait for `Stage 2` proof, even if that means buying far above the lows.

## Freedom Calibration

- For `discretionary chart review`, speak in probabilities and explain which evidence is structural versus contextual.
- For `screeners` and `backtests`, keep the numeric thresholds above explicit and separate `stage`, `base count`, `entry type`, and `damage rules`; do not collapse them into one fuzzy score.
- If a chart is ambiguous between `cheat`, `low cheat`, and `power play`, prefer `skip` over a forced label. This style makes money by being selective, not by always having an opinion.
- If the user asks for automation, encode post-entry `damage detection` and `market-regime adjustment` logic; a pure entry scanner is not faithful to Minervini's real edge.
